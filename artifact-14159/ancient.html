ANCIENT & NON-LATIN SYSTEMS

Where language, ritual, and concealment become indistinguishable

Unlike modern cryptography, ancient systems rarely separate encryption from meaning. Many were not designed to protect information from enemies, but to veil it from the uninitiated. The act of reading itself was often conditional — dependent on cultural, religious, or philosophical literacy.

In these systems, secrecy is not enforced by complexity alone, but by worldview. To understand the cipher is to understand the cosmology that produced it.

Atbash (Hebrew Reversal)

Atbash is frequently introduced as a simple monoalphabetic substitution: the first letter maps to the last, the second to the second-to-last, and so on. Aleph becomes Tav. Bet becomes Shin. On paper, this is trivial.

In practice, Atbash is inseparable from Hebrew philosophical thought. The Hebrew alphabet is not merely phonetic; it is numeric, symbolic, and cosmological. To reverse it is not simply to obscure meaning, but to invert creation itself.

שָׁלוֹם → פָּאֵם

Several rabbinical texts use Atbash not to hide forbidden ideas, but to gesture at them. The reader is meant to recognize the inversion and consider why it was necessary. This is not secrecy — it is discretion.

Observation (2012): Atbash assumes reverence for the alphabet. Without that, it collapses into a toy cipher.

Greek Isopsephy

Isopsephy assigns numeric values to Greek letters, allowing words and phrases to be evaluated as sums. This practice predates cryptography as we define it, yet it frequently functions as one.

Two phrases with equal numeric value are said to be equivalent in some deeper sense. This allows ideas to be encoded without substitution, transposition, or keys — only arithmetic.

ΛΟΓΟΣ = 373

What fascinates me is that isopsephy resists brute force. Without cultural context, numeric equivalence reveals nothing. With context, it reveals too much.

Later note (2017): Modern hash collisions feel philosophically similar, though stripped of intent.

Runic Substitution

Runes appear across Europe in contexts that defy classification. Sometimes they are phonetic. Sometimes symbolic. Occasionally, they are simply Latin letters written differently.

In several manuscripts, Latin prayers are rendered entirely in runes. This does not encrypt the text — it recontextualizes it. Literacy becomes a gate.

ᚠᚢᚦᚨᚱ

Runic substitution is best understood as a social cipher. It signals belonging. Those who can read it do not merely decode — they participate.

Acrophonic Systems

Acrophonic systems assign meaning based on the initial sound or symbol of a concept rather than a fixed alphabet. Early Greek numerals, Egyptian tallies, and several Near Eastern scripts rely on this principle.

These systems resist translation because they encode thought rather than sound. To read them mechanically is to misunderstand them.

Failure record: All attempts to algorithmically decode acrophonic inscriptions have produced convincing but false readings.

Cipher vs. Sacred Text

The modern distinction between encrypted text and sacred text is artificial. Many ancient works operate as both simultaneously. Meaning is revealed through initiation, repetition, and study — not keys.

I increasingly suspect that some texts labeled "religious" are better understood as cryptographic systems whose threat model was ignorance, not adversaries.

Unresolved question: If meaning requires belief, is disbelief a form of cryptanalysis?
