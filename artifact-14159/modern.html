MODERN & TRANSITIONAL SYSTEMS

Where mathematics succeeded and people failed

Modern cryptographic systems are frequently described as unbreakable. This is a convenient fiction. What is usually meant is that the mathematics resists direct attack. The systems fail elsewhere.

Unlike ancient or classical ciphers, modern systems rarely fail due to elegance or symbolism. They fail due to implementation, misunderstanding, impatience, and the persistent refusal to accept inconvenience in exchange for security.

Shift in perspective (2015): Attacking algorithms is inefficient. Observing humans is not.

XOR and Symmetric Operations

Exclusive OR is mathematically trivial and conceptually perfect. Encryption and decryption are identical operations. No information is destroyed. Nothing is hidden permanently.

XOR fails catastrophically when keys are reused. This is not a theoretical weakness. It is a documented certainty. Every dataset I have examined that used XOR at scale reused keys.

3a 27 2f 36 2c 7f 1d 0b
Case summary: Two messages, same key. Both recovered. No exception.

One-Time Pads

The one-time pad is provably secure. This fact is often cited with reverence. It is also irrelevant.

A system that requires perfect randomness, absolute secrecy, and strict single-use keys does not survive contact with reality. Pads are reused. Pads are copied. Pads are lost.

Repeated observation: The more secure a system is in theory, the less likely it is to be used correctly.

Encoding Mistaken for Encryption

Base64, hexadecimal, and similar encodings are not encryption. This distinction is obvious and endlessly ignored.

I have cataloged numerous cases where encoded data was described as "secure" solely because it appeared unfamiliar. Obscurity continues to masquerade as protection.

U0VDUkVUUw==
Decoded repeatedly. Confidence remained unjustified.

Key Management Failures

Modern systems depend less on clever algorithms and more on procedural discipline. Key rotation, storage, revocation — these are mundane tasks. They are also where most systems collapse.

In reviewing breaches, the pattern is monotonous. Keys embedded in code. Keys shared across environments. Keys never changed.

Personal frustration: The same mistakes reappear regardless of tooling.

On Trust

Modern cryptography assumes adversaries with infinite patience and computing power, yet routinely trusts users with secrets they cannot be expected to protect.

This contradiction is not accidental. Security is marketed as a feature, not a practice. Responsibility is shifted downward.\p>

Conclusion (provisional): The strongest cryptosystems fail quietly, betrayed by those they protect.
